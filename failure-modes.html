Failure Mode Catalog

The Failure Mode Catalog documents recurring patterns that reduce transparency in online rental listings. Each failure mode describes a specific way housing information can become incomplete, delayed, fragmented, or difficult to compare.

Purpose

In human systems engineering, failure modes help identify where a system breaks down for users. In this project, the system is the online rental search process. The renter is expected to evaluate affordability, eligibility, and housing fit, but the information needed to make those decisions is not always presented clearly.

This catalog translates observed listing problems into a structured taxonomy that can support evaluation, redesign, and future research.

Catalog

FMC-101: Price Decoupling

Advertised rent is presented separately from mandatory recurring fees, causing the displayed price to appear lower than the renter's likely monthly obligation.

Example: A listing advertises $1,650 rent but separates parking, trash, technology, and utility fees.

Risk: Renters may underestimate monthly costs when comparing listings.

FMC-102: Qualification Gating

Income, credit, guarantor, or screening requirements are hidden until late in the rental process.

Example: A renter does not see the income requirement until after beginning an application.

Risk: Renters may pay application fees for units they are unlikely to qualify for.

FMC-103: Concession Masking

Temporary promotions or discounts are emphasized more strongly than the long-term recurring cost of the unit.

Example: “One month free” is visually prominent, while the actual post-concession rent is less clear.

Risk: Renters may misjudge affordability after the promotional period ends.

FMC-104: Fee Fragmentation

Required fees are scattered across multiple pages, expandable sections, documents, or leasing steps.

Example: Application fees, parking fees, utility fees, and deposits appear in different locations.

Risk: Renters must do extra work to calculate total cost accurately.

FMC-105: Information Overload

Important housing information is technically available but buried in dense text, long documents, or visually cluttered layouts.

Example: Fee details are included in a long leasing document without a clear summary.

Risk: Renters may miss critical information even when it is disclosed.

Connection to the Housing Transparency Standard

Each failure mode reflects a breakdown in one or more layers of the Housing Transparency Standard. For example, price decoupling and fee fragmentation are failures of financial transparency, while qualification gating is a failure of eligibility transparency.

Design implication

Reducing these failure modes requires more than adding information to a page. Housing platforms should summarize total monthly cost, upfront move-in cost, and qualification requirements in a way that supports renter decision-making before the application process begins.
