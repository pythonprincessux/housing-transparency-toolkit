About the Research

This project investigates how rental housing websites present cost, qualification, and lease information to renters. The goal is to understand how unclear disclosures affect decision-making and to design tools that support more transparent housing choices.

Research problem

Online rental listings often advertise a base rent while separating or delaying information about mandatory fees, utilities, deposits, parking, application costs, and qualification requirements. As a result, renters may begin the application process without fully understanding the true monthly cost or whether they qualify for the unit.

This creates a human systems problem: renters must make high-stakes financial decisions using incomplete, fragmented, or difficult-to-find information.

Research question

How can human-centered design improve transparency during the online apartment search process?

Research objectives

  • Identify common transparency failures in online rental listings.
  • Develop a framework for evaluating housing information transparency.
  • Examine how renters interpret cost, fee, and qualification information.
  • Translate research findings into practical design tools for renters.

Methodology

The study uses a research-through-design approach that combines artifact analysis, rubric-based evaluation, failure mode identification, and usability-informed design recommendations.

  1. Artifact analysis: Rental listings were reviewed for cost, fee, utility, and qualification disclosures.
  2. Transparency rubric: Listings were evaluated using a consistent 0–3 scoring framework.
  3. Failure mode catalog: Recurring disclosure problems were organized into a taxonomy.
  4. Usability study: Participant feedback was used to understand how renters interpret housing information.
  5. Design intervention: Findings informed the Housing Transparency Toolkit and True-Cost Estimator concept.

Thesis artifact

The Housing Transparency Toolkit serves as the public-facing design artifact for this honors thesis. It translates the Housing Transparency Standard, Transparency Evaluation Rubric, Failure Mode Catalog, and True-Cost Estimator concept into accessible resources for renters, students, and early-career adults.

Why this matters

Housing decisions are financial, practical, and deeply personal. When important information is hidden or scattered, renters may underestimate costs, apply to units they cannot qualify for, or compare listings inaccurately.

Improving transparency can help renters better estimate affordability, avoid unnecessary application costs, and make decisions with clearer expectations.
