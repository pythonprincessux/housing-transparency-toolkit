Research Findings

This page summarizes the key findings from the Housing Transparency Toolkit research project. The findings show how hidden costs, fragmented information, and unclear qualification requirements can affect renter decision-making.

Finding 1: Advertised rent does not always reflect true monthly cost

Rental listings often emphasize base rent while separating mandatory monthly fees such as parking, utilities, trash, technology, amenity, or pet-related charges.

This creates a gap between the price renters first see and the amount they may actually owe each month.

Finding 2: Fee information is often fragmented

Cost information may appear across several parts of a listing, including expandable menus, application pages, fee disclosures, leasing documents, or third-party property websites.

This makes comparison harder because renters must manually gather and calculate costs from multiple locations.

Finding 3: Qualification requirements are not always visible early enough

Income, credit, guarantor, and screening requirements are not always presented before renters begin the application process.

When eligibility information is delayed, renters may spend time or money applying for housing they may not qualify for.

Finding 4: Participants preferred summarized cost information

Participants responded positively to interfaces that consolidated monthly costs and move-in costs into one clear summary.

This finding informed the True-Cost Estimator prototype, which demonstrates how recurring expenses can be presented as a single estimated monthly obligation.

Finding 5: Transparency supports better comparison

When listings clearly disclose fees, utilities, deposits, and qualification criteria, renters can compare options more accurately.

Transparent listings reduce the cognitive burden placed on renters and support more informed financial decisions.

Design implications

  • Rental platforms should display true monthly cost near advertised rent.
  • Required fees should be summarized before application.
  • Qualification requirements should be visible before renters submit personal information.
  • Move-in costs should be separated from recurring monthly costs.
  • Plain language summaries should be used for complex leasing information.

How the findings shaped the toolkit

The research findings directly informed the Housing Transparency Standard, Transparency Evaluation Rubric, Failure Mode Catalog, True-Cost Estimator, and renter-facing checklist.

Together, these tools translate the study's findings into practical resources for evaluating and improving rental housing transparency.
